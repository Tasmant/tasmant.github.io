Stoner

John Williams · 1965 ★★★★☆ Read: April 2025

This is a novel about a man who teaches literature at a mid-western university, marries the wrong person, and dies quietly. That's it. And somehow it is devastating.

William Stoner comes from a farm family, stumbles into a love of literature in college, and never leaves academia. His life contains no great drama — a bad marriage, a brief affair, a petty feud with a colleague. Williams renders all of it with such clear, unhurried prose that the ordinary weight of a life starts to feel immense.

I've read that this book was largely ignored when it came out and only found its audience decades later. That feels right, somehow. It's not a book that announces itself. It asks you to slow down and pay attention to the things that don't usually get novels written about them.

One star off only because the middle section drags slightly — the departmental conflict with Lomax goes on a bit long. But the final pages are among the best I've read in a while.
